Holistic Reflection

Use of Large Language Models, or Generative AI, is by far the most prominent issue in the public consciousness related to computer science today. To not have some opinion on it, as a graduating computer science major in the present year, would be a dereliction of your duty as a learned member of society. Its implications on workflow, as an “industry disruptor”, and on artistic creativity, are all hotly contested and worth some additional thought. My time learning computer science, and dealing with the early effects of this technology on learning at the job market, have given me a lot to say.

As a workflow enhancer, tools like Deepseek or Claude do have some benefits. They can create massive amounts of code at the push of a button, but with varying degrees of functionality. Sometimes these tools can single-handedly solve an entire project, but on further examination even when their code output works without error it is frequently a brute force solution to a given problem. I’ve never seen an AI produce code I would describe as beautiful. At best, it simply works. More often it has the syntactical structure of proper code, but with the “emphasis on the wrong syllable” that makes it… wrong somehow. And because its code is so good at looking on first examination like good code and making so much code so quickly, the would-be vibe-coding developer will find themself swiftly buried in deeply rooted issues they don’t bother to understand because they’re swimming against the river of hundreds or thousands of lines of code. Some of the best learning I have ever had in this major was through the act of bug-fixing errors for assignments, because I had a hand in the entire code base of those assignments and so could more easily parse what piece of code has what function. Moreover, that eureka moment at creating an elegant design is something that a prompt to Claude could never replace.

This dovetails into learning I’ve had in relation to my Digital Media Arts courses throughout my time at St. Thomas. So-called “AI Art” is a hot topic because of concerns of plagiarism, or stealing the work of “real creatives”, but more saliently, setting aside those issues, the output is often pretty bad. My visual media class in particular had an eye-opening assignment of giving us a one-two week crash course on photography, then had us take some photos with intentionality, and then get an AI model to generate similar images. The outcome was recognizing that a novice artist would frequently have worse lighting, occasionally worse framing of shots, but the novice’s intention still shone through in comparison to the flat AI shots. This carries through into computer science, because images aren’t the only medium in which AI is attempting to poorly do art. What drew me to computer science was a love of intricate systems, learning what makes them tick, learning how to integrate them together into a larger whole, finding the strange efficiencies and even stranger inefficiencies that come from thousands of artisans piling ideas on top of each other, and improving on each other's designs. That is a form of art. It could be considered a kind of poetry, or perhaps it's a whole different field, but computer science is as much a creative field as any other.

This is, I think, a minority position. I hear the grumblings from my peers for being forced to take general education courses, and specifically taking the humanities. Even the university itself creates this sense of distance, surrounding the Computer Scientists with the chemists, geologists, actuarial mathematicians, and physicists. The “hard sciences”, kept at arms length across the street from the social sciences or (heaven forbid) the humanities. No wonder, then, that in the place of having The Humanities in computer science we’re trying to altogether remove the Humanity from computer science. The AI tool that is now embedded in my word-processor-of-choice, in lieu of the entirely functioning spellcheck, claims that the previous sentence of this paper is an error. It’s endemic of the larger issue: functionality, or the appearance of such—rather than any interest in the artistry of writing, the poetry of language. The “large language model” is mere regression to the mean, the homogenization of thought. It sits in direct opposition to the university’s stated goals of academic excellence and diversity.

The popular pro-AI position frames it as a tool, like a compass is to a sketch artist. The more evangelist position frames it as socialist revolution—returning creative power over the most powerful medium to the people from the ivory-towered intelligentsia. By contrast, I have found AI to be a blight. The handful of times I’ve attempted to use it in my work I find myself not proud of the output, both in that I know it isn’t my best and that it doesn’t feel totally mine because there are inner workings I’m not fully cognizant of. It feels less like being an artist, and more like a janitor, cleaning up somebody else’s trash from a party. I used Deepseek for the frontend of a database assignment, and even though it was totally allowed I didn’t feel happy at the end. The frontend wasn’t the point, it was an afterthought for one extra credit point, but I know I could have done better with a mere two hours. I swore I would never do so again, but I was left feeling that I had lost my sense of dignity, and strayed from a pursuit of truth. You suggested that we could reflect on “ethical” AI, and while I’m sure I could produce some trite middle-of-the-road answer that “it’s a good tool, but it has to be used with care and caution”, I have learned only to see it as a moral rot. It aims to take the creativity out of creation, and in so doing lose track of the last and most important of St. Thomas’ convictions: “We celebrate the achievements of all members of our community in goals attained and obstacles overcome, and in all things give praise to God”.
